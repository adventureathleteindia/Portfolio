The Opportunity

What triggered this: Nepal's coalition government collapsed in late 2025. The incoming Prime Minister's first act was a 100-point governance roadmap — built around one core idea: fix how government works, assign clear ownership to every process, and eliminate the approval layers that have paralysed public services for decades. That is Business Process Re-engineering by another name.

"Nepal has formally adopted Business Process Re-engineering to simplify and restructure public administration. Every ministry must now define its processes, name responsible officials, and report on performance indicators. The mandate is real — but no specialist BPM partner is guiding the implementation."

Source: Nepal Government 100-Point Governance Roadmap — Kathmandu Post, March 2026

Key Reforms Underway

  • 1 Ministries reduced to 17 within 30 days — to tackle administrative inefficiency from an "excessively large number of ministries." A Transition Roadmap will be prepared covering personnel, budget, and operational continuity. ↗ Kathmandu Post, March 2026
  • 2 Multi-level approvals capped at 3 layers — unnecessary processes scrapped, delays eliminated. Standards and monitoring mechanisms to be prepared or revised within 30 days. ↗ Kathmandu Post, March 2026
  • 3 BPR formally named as the implementation method — "Through Business Process Re-engineering (BPR), the overall service delivery mechanism will be simplified, made faster and more results-oriented." Each ministry must produce work plans specifying key tasks, timelines, responsible officials, and performance indicators. ↗ Kathmandu Post, March 2026
  • 4 "Restructuring and Management Secretariat" established directly under the Prime Minister's Office to oversee and coordinate the entire restructuring process across all ministries. ↗ Kathmandu Post, March 2026

WHY THIS TIME IS DIFFERENT

Nepal's reform history is full of good intentions that stalled. Three things make 2026 different: federal restructuring is constitutionally mandated (not optional), digital infrastructure investment is live (Nagarik App has created public expectation of change), and development funding is allocated and sitting — World Bank's Nepal portfolio is active. The reform has to happen. Politically, it cannot be reversed.

Action Plan

How We Move Forward

A phased approach — research first, then reach out

1
Research the opportunity and define the strategy
Before any outreach, we need to understand the landscape properly. This means: identifying active World Bank, ADB, and UNDP programmes in Nepal's governance space, understanding current tender cycles and procurement windows, mapping the key decision-makers inside Nepal's Restructuring Secretariat, and pinpointing exactly where Wardell can credibly step in. This research shapes everything else and avoids wasted outreach.
2
Approach through international development organisations — not Nepal's government directly
Direct cold outreach to a foreign government might not work — it is relationship-driven and slow. The more reliable entry point is through World Bank, ADB, or UNDP, who are already funding Nepal's reform and actively seeking delivery partners. How it works: the funding body runs a tender → Wardell responds as an implementing organisation → the funding body manages the government relationship. We never need a cold government introduction.
3
Request a meeting or presentation opportunity
Once we have identified the right contact — through a development organisation or an alternate route — the goal is a meeting or short presentation: who Wardell is, what we can deliver, and why now. Not a formal proposal yet. Just an initial conversation to gauge interest, understand the procurement path, and position ourselves before a tender opens.

No contracts, no travel, no commitment at this stage — just a green light to research and identify the right door to knock on.
